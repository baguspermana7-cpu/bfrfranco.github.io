Your Maintenance Compliance Problem Is Not a People Problem

Every facility manager I talk to says the same thing: "We need more disciplined technicians."

They are solving the wrong problem.

80% of maintenance failures come from planning, not execution. I have seen this pattern at facility after facility. The technician shows up. The parts are not there. The procedure references equipment that was replaced 3 years ago. The window conflicts with a change freeze nobody told them about.

And when the PM does not get done, it goes on their record.

One 15MW data center I worked with was stuck at 74% PM compliance. Leadership blamed technician discipline. The actual breakdown:

- 34% of failures: scheduling conflicts (overlapping windows, no resource leveling)
- 26%: parts/tool unavailability at time of work
- 22%: procedure mismatch with actual equipment configuration
- 18%: genuine execution issues

They fixed the system — not the people. 18 weeks later: 97.2% compliance. Zero headcount added.

The three changes that moved the needle most:

1. Resource leveling (no more than 3 concurrent PMs in a single shift)
2. Failure-mode-based scheduling (maintenance driven by degradation pattern, not calendar)
3. Visual management boards (physical, not just CMMS dashboards)

Your CMMS tracks schedule adherence. But does it track whether the PM actually detected what it was designed to detect?
