Power issues caused 54% of all data center outages in 2024. That number should terrify anyone running critical infrastructure.

I spent weeks researching how the biggest hyperscalers actually design their power distribution systems. Not the marketing slides -- the real engineering. And the differences between approaches are staggering.

AWS threw out the centralized UPS playbook entirely. They put small battery packs in every rack. The result? A single failure now impacts one rack instead of an entire data hall. That is an 89% reduction in blast radius. Their distributed micro-UPS cuts conversion losses by 35% and achieves six nines availability.

Google went even further with server-level batteries. They have deployed over 100 million Li-ion cells across their fleet. Their move to 48V DC distribution reduced power losses by 16x compared to the old 12V standard. Fleet PUE: 1.09.

Microsoft, working with Meta and Google, created the Mt Diablo architecture -- a disaggregated power approach using sidecar power racks with 400V DC. This enables 15-35% more AI accelerators per rack and scales from 100 kW to 1 MW per rack.

Then there is xAI Colossus in Memphis. 2 GW total capacity. That is 40% of Memphis's average daily energy usage in a single facility. They installed 168 Tesla Megapacks and use 1.3 million gallons per day of cooling water.

The voltage evolution tells the bigger story. Going from 12V to 48V DC cuts distribution losses by 93.75%. The next frontier is 800V DC, which NVIDIA is pushing with its sidecar architecture for MW-scale racks -- reducing copper requirements by 16.7x versus 48V.

Meanwhile, Anthropic is doing something fundamentally different: distributing across AWS, Google Cloud, Azure, and Fluidstack simultaneously. Over 2 GW aggregated across four providers, three chip architectures, six states, and four independent grid operators. No single provider failure takes them down completely.
