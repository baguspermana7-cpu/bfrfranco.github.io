Imagine a chemical so persistent it will still be detectable in your bloodstream 50 years from now. Now imagine it evaporates into the air every time engineers do routine maintenance on AI cooling systems — and nobody is required to measure it.

That's not hypothetical. That's the current state of two-phase PFAS cooling in data centers.

I've spent 12 years as a data center engineer. I've opened these systems hundreds of times. The fluid inside evaporates eight times faster than water the moment it hits open air. Every maintenance session is a release event. We just never called it that, because nobody asked us to track it.

Here's the stat that stopped me: maintenance vapor releases put 20 to 30 times more PFAS into the environment than sealed-system leaks. The leaks everyone worries about are not the main problem. Opening the tank is.

And the regulatory picture: over 12,000 known PFAS compounds. Mandatory environmental reporting for data centers that use them — zero. The rules apply to manufacturers, not the facilities doing the handling.

The industry's answer was to switch to "PFAS-free" alternatives. The leading replacement degrades in the environment into trifluoroacetic acid — also persistent, also unregulated. The label changed. The chemistry didn't.

And most data centers didn't even need PFAS cooling. Two-phase systems make sense above 100 kW per rack. Under 5% of facilities reach that. The rest adopted it during the AI boom, took on up to $1 million in disposal liability for large systems, and put nothing on the balance sheet.
