Question: Are data centers contributing to PFAS contamination?

---

Yes — and the primary pathway is one the industry has largely avoided discussing.

The common assumption is that PFAS contamination from data centers, if it exists, would come from coolant leaks: a failed seal, a cracked line, fluid pooling somewhere it shouldn't. That happens, but it's a relatively minor source. The much larger contamination vector is what happens during routine maintenance.

Two-phase cooling systems — used for high-density AI and GPU computing infrastructure — rely on PFAS-based dielectric fluids. Novec 7000 is one of the most common. It has a vapor pressure of 270 hPa, compared to water's 32 hPa. That means it evaporates roughly eight times faster than water at room temperature. When a technician opens a two-phase system to inspect hardware, swap a component, or top off fluid levels, the exposed fluid surface begins vaporizing immediately into the facility air. Studies and engineering assessments indicate that maintenance releases generate 20 to 30 times more PFAS than sealed-system leaks over the same operational period.

The regulatory gap is significant. The EPA's Toxic Release Inventory requires PFAS reporting from manufacturers. It does not require data center operators — who buy, store, handle, and periodically vent these chemicals — to measure or report what they release. There are currently over 12,000 identified PFAS compounds, and zero require mandatory environmental reporting from the facilities that use them operationally.

The "PFAS-free" transition makes this more complicated, not less. After 3M exited PFAS manufacturing at the end of 2025, the industry pivoted toward HFO-based alternatives like Opteon 2P50, marketed as next-generation and environmentally responsible. The problem: HFOs degrade in the environment into trifluoroacetic acid (TFA). TFA is persistent in water, shows bioaccumulation potential, and is currently classified outside most existing PFAS regulatory frameworks — meaning it isn't counted in contamination assessments even when it's present. The industry replaced one class of persistent compounds with a degradation product of another, and reclassified the problem rather than solving it.

There is also a deployment question worth raising. Two-phase PFAS cooling is justified at rack densities above roughly 100 kW. Under five percent of data centers operate at that threshold. The majority adopted the technology for competitive differentiation during the AI infrastructure build-out, taking on PFAS disposal liability — currently $8 to $15 per liter — without the thermal requirement that would have justified it. A 100,000-liter system represents approximately $1 million in disposal cost that appears on no balance sheet today.

The short answer: yes, data centers are contributing to PFAS contamination. The mechanism is not dramatic — no catastrophic spill, no visible leak. It's incremental, routine, largely invisible, and almost entirely unregulated at the operator level.
