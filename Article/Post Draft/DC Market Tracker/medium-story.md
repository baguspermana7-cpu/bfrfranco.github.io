# 122 Gigawatts and Counting: Mapping the Global Data Center Boom

The global data center industry reached 122.2 gigawatts of installed capacity in 2025. Another 12.5 gigawatts is under active construction. Hyperscaler capital expenditure hit $413 billion in a single year, up 84 percent from 2024.

These numbers are remarkable on their own. But what makes them genuinely difficult to work with is that they are scattered across dozens of different reports, each covering a slightly different geography, using slightly different definitions, and updated on different schedules. CBRE covers primary US and European markets. JLL has broader global reach but publishes annually. Synergy Research tracks hyperscaler counts. Turner and Townsend covers construction costs. IEA covers power demand. Cushman and Wakefield covers Asia Pacific.

Getting a single coherent picture of where capacity exists, where it is being built, and where growth is heading requires cross-referencing all of them. I did that and built an interactive dashboard to make the result accessible.

## What the Tracker Shows

The Global DC Market Tracker covers 25 markets across 6 regions: North America, Europe, Asia Pacific, Latin America, Middle East, and Africa.

For each market, the dashboard shows operational capacity in MW, construction pipeline, planned future capacity, vacancy rate, wholesale colocation pricing per kW per month, average power cost per kWh, compound annual growth rate through 2030, and the top 4 players in each market.

The data is presented in three formats: an interactive SVG world map with bubbles sized by capacity and color-coded by market maturity, a filterable grid of market cards, and a sortable data table for detailed comparison.

## What the Data Reveals

Several patterns emerge when you see all the markets together rather than in isolation.

The market is more concentrated than most people realize. Northern Virginia alone accounts for over 4,000 MW of operational capacity with another 5,500 MW in planning. That single corridor has more data center infrastructure than most countries. The top 5 markets — Northern Virginia, London, Tokyo, Dallas, and Frankfurt — represent a disproportionate share of global capacity.

The growth story has shifted geography. The fastest growing markets by CAGR through 2030 are not the traditional powerhouses. Dubai, Nairobi, and Northern Virginia lead at 25 percent annual growth. Kuala Lumpur and Dallas follow at 22 percent. Mumbai and Phoenix at 20 percent. Jakarta at 18 percent.

Meanwhile, the established European hubs that defined the industry for the past decade are growing at single digits. Amsterdam at 8 percent, Dublin at 6 percent, Frankfurt at 10 percent. These markets face increasing constraints around power availability, environmental regulation, and community opposition to new builds.

Cost variance creates structural advantages. Construction costs per MW range from $7.5 million in Mumbai to $15.2 million in Tokyo, a 2x spread that reflects differences in labor costs, material supply chains, land prices, and regulatory complexity.

Colocation pricing tells a parallel story. Singapore at $390 per kW per month versus Mumbai at $125. The 3x spread reflects not just cost differences but supply constraints — Singapore has less than 1 percent vacancy, the tightest market in the world, while Mumbai has 6 percent vacancy with aggressive expansion plans.

Power cost is perhaps the most important long-term differentiator. Dallas at $0.055 per kWh versus Singapore at $0.18 per kWh creates a 3.3x operational cost difference that compounds across every MW-hour of operations, every year, for the life of the facility. For a 10 MW deployment, that difference amounts to millions of dollars annually.

## The AI Infrastructure Effect

The data center market in 2025 is fundamentally different from even two years ago because of AI infrastructure demand.

AI-ready facilities — those with liquid cooling, high-density power distribution, and GPU-optimized layouts — cost $20 million or more per MW to build, compared to the $10.7 million global average for conventional facilities. This premium reflects the additional cooling infrastructure, higher electrical density, and structural requirements for heavy GPU chassis.

Hyperscaler CAPEX tells the story most clearly. $413 billion in 2025 infrastructure spending, up 84 percent year-over-year. Much of this is directed at AI training and inference capacity. The pipeline of announced projects suggests this spending will continue accelerating through 2030, with total investment projected at $3 trillion across the decade.
