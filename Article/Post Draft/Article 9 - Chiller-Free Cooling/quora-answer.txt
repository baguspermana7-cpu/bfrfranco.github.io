QUORA ANSWER DRAFT
==================
Target Question: "Will AI data centers eliminate the need for chillers?"
(Also fits: "Does liquid cooling replace HVAC?", "Can Nvidia GPU liquid cooling work in tropical climates?", "What cooling does a 100kW rack need?")

---

ANSWER:

Short answer: No. And anyone telling you otherwise hasn't done the thermal math for tropical climates.

Here's the reality that gets lost in the hype around direct-to-chip liquid cooling:

Yes, Nvidia's Rubin/Blackwell GPUs with direct-to-chip cooling remove ~70-80% of the heat at the chip level. That's impressive. But it doesn't eliminate the remaining 20-30% of heat that still radiates into the room — from VRMs, NVLink switches, NVMe drives, power supplies, and network equipment.

In a 100 kW rack, that's 20-30 kW of residual heat per rack that still needs traditional air cooling.

Now add the tropical factor: outdoor ambient in Southeast Asia averages 28-35C with 70-90% relative humidity. Free cooling (using outdoor air directly) requires outdoor temps below ~18C to be effective. That means zero free cooling hours in most of SEA.

The liquid cooling loop itself needs heat rejection. Those rear-door heat exchangers or CDUs (Coolant Distribution Units) reject heat to... a cooling tower or dry cooler. In tropical climates with wet-bulb temps of 27-28C, you still need mechanical cooling (chillers) to get coolant temps low enough.

What actually changes:
- Chiller CAPACITY reduces (you're cooling 20-30% of heat, not 100%)
- Chiller TYPE may change (higher temp setpoints enable more efficient operation)
- PUE improves (from 1.3-1.5 down to 1.1-1.2)
- But HVAC doesn't disappear. It transforms.
