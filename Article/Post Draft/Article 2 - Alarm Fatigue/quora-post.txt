847 Alarms Before the Operator Took Off His Jacket

True story from a data center I worked with.

By 07:00, 63 new alarms arrived. The operator acknowledged them in batches because the screen was full. At 07:23, a real chiller fault triggered — buried under 34 downstream alarms. He saw it. He clicked acknowledge. He moved on.

By 09:15, the data hall hit 27C. The root fault sat there for 112 minutes.

Was this negligence? No. This operator followed a rational strategy for an irrational system. When 95% of alarms are known noise, you stop investigating each one. That is not complacency — cognitive science predicts exactly this behavior.

ISA-18.2 sets the limit: maximum 1 alarm per operator per 10-minute window. This facility was running 5.6-8.3. At 800+ alarms per day, the Poisson model gives a 99.5% probability of at least one alarm flood per 12-hour shift.

The question was never IF operators get overwhelmed. It was WHEN.

We ran a 10-week rationalization while the facility stayed fully operational:

Result after 90 days:
- 90%+ alarm reduction (800+ down to fewer than 80 per day)
- Response time: 180s to 45s (75% improvement)
- Zero missed critical alarms in 6 months
- Zero new staff hired

No new technology purchased. The system itself was the problem.

If your organization responds to missed alarms with retraining and disciplinary action, you are treating a symptom while reinforcing the root cause.
