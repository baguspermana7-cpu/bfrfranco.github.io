## Body

February 2026. The India AI Impact Summit in New Delhi. Sam Altman, CEO of the company behind ChatGPT, steps up to a panel and drops a statement that would ricochet across every newsroom and social media feed on the planet:

"The water thing is -- I think -- mostly fake. The newer data centers, they don't really use that much water."

Within hours, the clip went viral. Environmental groups fired back. Researchers who had spent years quantifying data center water usage were stunned. Community activists in Oregon, Arizona, and Alabama -- people who had watched their local water supplies get redirected into server farms -- felt gaslit.

But here is the thing: Altman was not entirely wrong. And he was not entirely right.

As a data center engineer with 12+ years in critical infrastructure, I have operated cooling towers, specified chiller plants, and watched real-time water meters tick upward. The truth about AI's water footprint is more nuanced than either side admits.

This is a fact-check. Every claim gets weighed against peer-reviewed research, corporate sustainability reports, and on-the-ground data.

### Claim 1: "The Water Thing Is Mostly Fake"

Verdict: Mostly False.

U.S. data centers consumed approximately 17 billion gallons of water in 2023, according to peer-reviewed research published in Joule. That is roughly equivalent to the annual water consumption of a city of 300,000 people. Projections show this reaching 68 billion gallons by 2028 -- a 4x increase driven by AI workloads.

These are not "fake" numbers.

### Claim 2: "Newer Data Centers Don't Use Much Water"

Verdict: Partially True, But Misleading.

Some purpose-built AI facilities do use closed-loop cooling with minimal water. Microsoft, Meta, and some hyperscalers are building newer facilities with air-cooled or closed-loop designs. But approximately 56% of data centers globally still use evaporative cooling -- water-hungry by design.

The majority of new capacity being built in 2025-2026 still uses evaporative cooling because it is cheaper, faster to deploy, and proven at scale.

### Claim 3: "Old Statistics Are Misleading"

Verdict: True -- Some Viral Claims ARE Misleading.

The viral claim that "a single ChatGPT query uses 17 gallons of water" is genuinely false. UC Riverside's peer-reviewed research shows the actual figure is approximately 10-50ml per query. Still significant at scale, but nothing close to gallons.

Altman is right about this one. The viral misstatement did real damage to the credibility of legitimate water concerns.

### The Corporate Reports Altman Does Not Mention

Microsoft's 2024 Environmental Sustainability Report revealed water consumption rose 34% from 2021 to 2022, directly attributed to "growth in AI research and cloud computing." Google's showed a 20% year-over-year increase, reaching approximately 6.1 billion liters in 2023.

If AI water usage is "mostly fake," someone forgot to tell Microsoft and Google's own sustainability teams.

### The Communities Nobody Asked

In The Dalles, Oregon, Google's data centers consume approximately 29% of the city's total water supply. In Mesa, Arizona, communities under drought restrictions are fighting new data center permits. In Bessemer, Alabama, a $14.5 billion data center campus requested 2 million gallons of water per day from a system that cannot provide it without significant upgrades.

### The Engineer's Bottom Line

Both things are true simultaneously. The industry IS improving. And the current situation IS a real problem.

What is not helpful is a tech CEO standing on a global stage and calling the concerns of researchers, communities, and his own company's sustainability reports "mostly fake." That is not engineering. That is PR.

The water data is not fake. The communities are not fake. And the 68 billion gallons projected by 2028 will not be fake either -- unless the industry takes the problem seriously enough to actually solve it.
