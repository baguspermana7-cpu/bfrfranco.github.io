Sam Altman recently told an audience at the India AI Impact Summit that water concerns about AI data centers are "mostly fake."

As someone who has spent 12+ years operating critical infrastructure — including the cooling systems that keep data centers running — I felt compelled to fact-check this claim against the peer-reviewed research.

Here is what the data actually says:

THE NUMBERS
- U.S. data centers consumed approximately 17 billion gallons of water in 2023 (peer-reviewed, published in Joule)
- Projections show this reaching 68 billion gallons by 2028 — a 4x increase driven primarily by AI workloads
- 56% of data centers globally still use evaporative cooling, which consumes significant fresh water
- Microsoft's own water consumption rose 34% from 2021-2022, attributed to "growth in AI research and cloud computing"
- Google's water usage has more than tripled since 2016

WHERE ALTMAN IS RIGHT
Some viral statistics ARE misleading. The claim that "a single ChatGPT query uses 17 gallons of water" is genuinely false. UC Riverside's peer-reviewed research shows the actual figure is approximately 10-50ml per query — significant at scale, but nothing close to gallons.

WHERE HE IS WRONG
Dismissing all water concerns as "fake" ignores:
- His own infrastructure partner's sustainability reports documenting massive year-over-year increases
- Communities like The Dalles, Oregon (where Google uses 29% of city water) and Mesa, Arizona (drought restrictions vs. new data center permits)
- The fact that even "water-efficient" designs undercount upstream water from power generation

THE ENGINEERING REALITY
The industry IS making progress. Closed-loop cooling, direct-to-chip systems, and heat reuse projects are technically elegant and genuinely water-efficient. But the installed base of water-cooled facilities will operate for 15-25 more years, and AI is driving unprecedented demand on that existing infrastructure.

Both things are true simultaneously: the industry is improving, AND the current situation is a real problem.

What is not helpful is dismissing the concerns of researchers, communities, and your own company's sustainability reports as "fake." That is not engineering. That is PR.
