# Quora Post

## Target Questions
- "Is Sam Altman right that AI water concerns are fake?"
- "How much water does ChatGPT use?"
- "Do data centers use a lot of water?"
- "What is the environmental impact of AI data centers?"

---

## Answer

I have been a data center engineer for over 12 years. I have operated cooling towers, specified chiller plants, and watched real-time water meters. When Sam Altman said at the India AI Impact Summit in February 2026 that water concerns about AI data centers are "mostly fake," I decided to fact-check every claim against peer-reviewed research.

Here is what I found:

Where Altman is right: Some viral statistics ARE genuinely misleading. The claim that "a single ChatGPT query uses 17 gallons of water" is false. UC Riverside's peer-reviewed research shows the actual figure is approximately 10-50ml per query. That is significant at scale, but nothing close to gallons.

Where Altman is wrong: Dismissing all water concerns as "fake" ignores substantial evidence:

1. U.S. data centers consumed approximately 17 billion gallons of water in 2023, per peer-reviewed research published in Joule.

2. That number is projected to reach 68 billion gallons by 2028 -- a 4x increase driven primarily by AI workloads.

3. Microsoft's own 2024 sustainability report documented a 34% increase in water consumption from 2021 to 2022, directly attributed to "growth in AI research and cloud computing." Microsoft is OpenAI's primary infrastructure partner.

4. Google's water consumption has more than tripled since 2016.

5. In The Dalles, Oregon, Google's data centers consume approximately 29% of the entire city's water supply.

6. Approximately 56% of data centers globally still use evaporative cooling, which consumes large volumes of fresh water.

The engineering reality is nuanced. The industry IS making progress with closed-loop cooling and direct-to-chip systems. But the installed base of water-cooled facilities will continue operating for 15-25 more years, and AI is driving unprecedented demand on that existing infrastructure.

Both things are true: the industry is improving, AND the current situation is a real problem. What is not helpful is dismissing peer-reviewed research and community concerns as "fake."

I wrote a comprehensive fact-check with a full verdict table, community impact analysis, and all sources cited: https://resistancezero.com/article-20.html

Sources: UC Riverside research (Joule), Microsoft 2024 Environmental Sustainability Report, Google 2024 Environmental Report, The Dalles city water records, Xylem/Global Water Intelligence 2026.
