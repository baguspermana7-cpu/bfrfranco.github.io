# Quora Post

Target Questions:
1. Will data centers cause power grid failures?
2. Why are electricity prices increasing because of AI?
3. What is PJM Interconnection?
4. Can the US power grid handle AI data centers?
5. Are data centers bad for the electricity grid?

---

## Answer

I work in data center operations and this is a question I deal with professionally. The short answer is yes, data center power demand is creating a genuine grid reliability concern — and PJM Interconnection is the most critical example.

PJM operates the largest power grid in North America, serving 65 million people across 13 states and Washington, DC. By 2027-2028, PJM projects a potential 6 gigawatt capacity shortfall where demand could exceed available supply.

The core problem has three components.

First, data centers account for 30 to 40 percent of PJM's projected load growth. Dominion Energy in Virginia has received data center power requests totaling over 30 gigawatts — more than its entire current system peak of 21 gigawatts.

Second, approximately 40 gigawatts of coal and aging gas plants are at risk of retirement by 2030. These retirements are driven by economics and EPA regulations. The replacement pipeline has 250 gigawatts of renewables in PJM's queue, but less than 5 percent of queued projects actually get built, and solar does not provide the same reliable capacity as baseload thermal plants.

Third, the grid was designed for a fundamentally different demand profile. Data centers need power 24/7 at high load factors. Traditional grid planning assumed moderate growth and predictable seasonal peaks.

How real is the risk? In December 2022, Winter Storm Elliott caused PJM to lose 46 gigawatts through forced outages. Rolling blackouts were minutes away across the entire 13-state footprint. The capacity auction that followed saw prices spike 800 percent — from $28.92 to $269.92 per megawatt-day.

The nuclear option is emerging as a solution. Microsoft is spending $1.6 billion to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1 (837 MW). Amazon acquired a data center campus next to the Susquehanna nuclear plant for $650 million. Nuclear provides the 24/7 zero-carbon baseload that matches data center demand profiles perfectly.

For consumers, the impact is real. Capacity cost increases translate to approximately $2-5 per month more on residential bills. Industrial users face larger increases.

The honest answer from an infrastructure perspective: we cannot build unlimited data centers without proportional investment in grid infrastructure. The data center industry needs to contribute its fair share to grid upgrades, not just consume capacity that was built for residential and commercial customers.
