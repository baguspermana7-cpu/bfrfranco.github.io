The largest power grid in North America is heading for a capacity crisis — and data centers are at the center of it.

PJM Interconnection serves 65 million people across 13 states and Washington, DC. It operates 180 GW of installed capacity. And by 2027-2028, PJM projects a potential 6 GW shortfall where demand could exceed available supply.

As a data center engineer managing critical infrastructure, this is the problem that keeps me up at night. Our facilities depend on grid reliability. UPS systems and generators buy us hours, not days. If the grid fails, we fail.

THE DATA
- Data centers account for 30-40% of PJM's projected load growth
- Dominion Energy Virginia has 30+ GW of DC power requests — exceeding its entire current system peak of 21 GW
- PJM capacity auction prices spiked 800% in one year ($28 to $269/MW-day)
- Total capacity procurement cost jumped from $2.2B to $14.7B
- 40 GW of thermal generation is at risk of retirement by 2030
- Winter Storm Elliott (2022): PJM lost 46 GW, came within minutes of rolling blackouts

THE NUCLEAR PLAY
Microsoft is restarting Three Mile Island Unit 1 (837 MW) with a $1.6B, 20-year PPA from Constellation Energy. Amazon acquired a data center campus adjacent to the Susquehanna nuclear plant for $650M (960 MW). Nuclear is the only zero-carbon baseload source that runs 24/7, matching DC demand profiles perfectly.

But FERC rejected Amazon's co-location deal over cost-shifting concerns. The question is legitimate: if a data center secures dedicated nuclear power behind the meter, does that reduce available supply for everyone else?

THE POLITICAL BATTLEFIELD
- Utilities want data centers to pay for grid upgrades
- Environmentalists oppose new gas generation
- Grid operators say renewables alone can't replace retiring baseload fast enough
- State regulators are questioning DC tax incentives
- PJM approved $5.1B in transmission upgrades, but construction takes years

WHAT THIS MEANS
We are building the most power-hungry technology in human history on a grid designed for a fundamentally different demand profile. PJM's challenge is a preview of what every major grid will face.

The data center industry needs to be honest about its power footprint and contribute proportionally to grid infrastructure investment. We cannot build infinite data centers without grid investment. Reliability is not someone else's problem — it is our problem.
