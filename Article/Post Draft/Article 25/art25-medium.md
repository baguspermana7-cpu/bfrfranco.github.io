Most people have never heard of PJM Interconnection. That is understandable. PJM does not advertise. It does not have a consumer brand. But PJM operates the largest power grid in North America, serving 65 million people across 13 states and Washington, DC. From Virginia to Illinois, from Pennsylvania to North Carolina, 180 gigawatts of installed capacity hums along under PJM's coordination.

And it is heading for a crisis.

## The 6 Gigawatt Gap

PJM's own load forecast, updated in January 2024, projected demand growth of approximately 40 gigawatts by 2039. This was a radical departure from previous forecasts that assumed relatively flat demand. The primary driver: data centers and electrification.

By the 2027-2028 delivery year, PJM's models show a scenario where available capacity may not meet projected peak demand plus the required reserve margin of 14.7%. The gap — approximately 6 gigawatts — is not academic. Six gigawatts is enough to power 4.5 million homes.

The North American Electric Reliability Corporation flagged PJM as an "elevated risk" area in its Long-Term Reliability Assessment. This is the entity whose sole purpose is monitoring grid reliability. When NERC raises a flag, the concern is real.

## Data Centers Are 40% of the Problem

Data centers account for 30 to 40 percent of PJM's projected load growth. The concentration is most extreme in Virginia. Dominion Energy, the utility serving the Ashburn data center corridor — the largest cluster of data centers in the world — has received interconnection requests from data center operators totaling over 30 gigawatts. Dominion's entire current system peak is approximately 21 gigawatts.

Read that again. One utility has received power requests from one industry that exceed its total capacity. The math does not work without massive infrastructure investment.

PJM's broader interconnection queue holds 250 gigawatts of pending projects, mostly renewable generation and battery storage. But fewer than 5 percent of queued projects reach completion. The average time from application to interconnection is four to five years.

## The 800% Auction Shock

The clearest signal came in July 2024, when PJM held its Base Residual Auction for the 2025-2026 delivery year. Capacity prices cleared at $269.92 per megawatt-day. The previous auction had cleared at $28.92 per megawatt-day. That is a nearly 800 percent increase in one year.

Total procurement cost jumped from approximately $2.2 billion to $14.7 billion. This is the market mechanism through which PJM ensures enough generation exists to meet projected demand. When prices spike this dramatically, the market is communicating a simple message: supply is no longer keeping pace with demand.

For consumers, this translates to higher electricity bills. Estimates range from $2 to $5 per month for residential customers, with larger increases for industrial and commercial users. In a region where energy affordability is already a concern, this adds fuel to growing political opposition to data center development.

## 40 Gigawatts of Retirements

On the supply side, approximately 40 gigawatts of thermal generation — primarily coal and aging natural gas — is at risk of retirement within PJM's territory by 2030. EPA regulations on mercury emissions, the Good Neighbor Plan for interstate pollution, and proposed carbon rules are accelerating coal retirements. Aging natural gas plants face economics that often do not justify the capital expenditure required for continued operation.

The replacement pipeline is theoretically large — 250 gigawatts of renewables in the queue. But renewable generation and dispatchable thermal generation are not equivalent from a reliability perspective. PJM uses Effective Load Carrying Capability to calculate how much reliable capacity each resource type actually provides. A gigawatt of solar generates power only during daylight hours and at reduced output on cloudy days. Under ELCC methodology, 1 GW of solar may provide only 15 to 30 percent of that value as reliable capacity.

The math is unforgiving: retirements are happening on a known schedule, but replacements are uncertain in both timing and effective capacity.

## The Nuclear Lifeline

In September 2024, Constellation Energy announced it would restart Three Mile Island Unit 1, an 837-megawatt nuclear reactor that had been shut down since 2019. Microsoft signed a 20-year power purchase agreement for 100 percent of the plant's output. Estimated cost: $1.6 billion. Target restart: 2028.

In a separate deal, Amazon acquired Talen Energy's Cumulus Data Campus adjacent to the 2.5-gigawatt Susquehanna nuclear station. The $650 million deal created a 960-megawatt data center campus with direct nuclear power through behind-the-meter co-location.

FERC rejected the original interconnection agreement for the Amazon-Talen deal in November 2024, citing concerns that co-location arrangements could shift grid costs to remaining ratepayers.

Nuclear provides exactly what data centers need: zero-carbon, 24/7 baseload power with extremely high capacity factors. The debate is not whether nuclear works for data centers — it clearly does. The debate is whether dedicating nuclear output to data centers reduces available supply for everyone else.

## What Happens If PJM Fails

The consequences of a major PJM failure are not hypothetical. In December 2022, Winter Storm Elliott caused PJM to lose 46 gigawatts of generation capacity through forced outages. The system came within minutes of implementing rolling blackouts across its 13-state footprint.

Texas provides the most vivid analog. Winter Storm Uri in February 2021 caused cascading failures across ERCOT, leaving millions without power for days. The death toll reached 246. The economic damage exceeded $195 billion. And ERCOT serves fewer people than PJM.

A comparable failure in PJM would affect hospitals, water treatment facilities, transportation systems, financial infrastructure, and tens of millions of homes. PJM is also interconnected with neighboring grids, meaning a failure could cascade beyond its boundaries.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

We are building the most power-hungry technology in human history on a grid that was designed for a fundamentally different demand profile. The data center industry needs to reckon with this reality.

Data centers cannot exist as free riders on public infrastructure. They consume 40 percent of the growth. They should contribute proportionally to the grid investment required to serve that growth. The current model — where data centers secure tax incentives to locate in a jurisdiction and then consume disproportionate shares of grid capacity — is not sustainable.

The solutions exist: grid-scale battery storage, nuclear restarts and new SMR construction, demand response programs, massive transmission upgrades, and reformed interconnection processes. But they require time, capital, and political will.

PJM's challenge is a preview of what every major grid will face as AI infrastructure scales. The question is whether we plan for it or wait for the blackout.
