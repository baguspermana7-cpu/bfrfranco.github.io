# YouTube / TikTok Script — PJM Grid Crisis

Duration: 7-9 minutes
Style: Talking head + data graphics + grid maps
Tone: Urgent but analytical — engineer explaining a crisis, not fear-mongering

---

## HOOK (0:00 - 0:30)

65 million people. 13 states. One power grid. And it's running out of electricity.

PJM Interconnection is the largest grid operator in North America. Most people have never heard of it. But if it fails, hospitals go dark, water treatment stops, and tens of millions lose power.

By 2027, PJM faces a 6 gigawatt shortfall. And data centers are consuming 40% of the growth.

I'm a data center engineer. This is the crisis nobody's talking about.

---

## SECTION 1: WHAT IS PJM? (0:30 - 1:30)

[Map graphic showing PJM territory]

PJM Interconnection manages the power grid across 13 states — Virginia, Pennsylvania, Ohio, Maryland, New Jersey, and more — plus Washington DC. 180 gigawatts of capacity. 65 million people.

It's headquartered in Valley Forge, Pennsylvania. You've never heard of it because when it works, nobody notices. But when it fails — and it almost did in December 2022 — the consequences affect everyone.

During Winter Storm Elliott, PJM lost 46 gigawatts of generation. Rolling blackouts were minutes away. That was a preview of what's coming.

---

## SECTION 2: THE 6 GW GAP (1:30 - 3:00)

[Data visualization: supply vs demand projection]

Three things are colliding at once.

Number one: 40 gigawatts of coal and gas plants are retiring or at risk of retirement by 2030. Old plants, bad economics, EPA regulations.

Number two: data centers are consuming 40 percent of load growth. Dominion Energy in Virginia alone has received 30 gigawatts of data center power requests. Their entire current system peak is 21 gigawatts.

[Text overlay: 30 GW requested vs 21 GW total system]

Number three: replacements can't be built fast enough. PJM has 250 gigawatts of projects in its queue, but less than 5 percent actually get built. Average wait: 4-5 years.

The result? By 2027-2028, demand may exceed supply. The gap: approximately 6 gigawatts.

---

## SECTION 3: THE 800% PRICE SPIKE (3:00 - 4:30)

[Auction price chart]

Here's how you know the crisis is real: follow the money.

In July 2024, PJM held its annual capacity auction. This is how they ensure enough power plants exist to meet demand.

Previous year: $28.92 per megawatt-day.
This year: $269.92 per megawatt-day.

That's an 800 percent increase. Total cost went from 2.2 billion to 14.7 billion dollars.

[Text overlay: $2.2B → $14.7B]

Your electricity bill will reflect this. Estimated impact: $2-5 more per month for residential customers. More for businesses.

The market is screaming: supply cannot keep up with demand.

---

## SECTION 4: THE NUCLEAR SOLUTION (4:30 - 6:00)

[B-roll: nuclear plant, data center]

So what's the solution? The biggest data center operators think it's nuclear.

Microsoft is spending $1.6 billion to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1 — an 837 megawatt reactor that's been shut down since 2019. They signed a 20-year deal for all of its output. Target restart: 2028.

Amazon acquired a data center campus right next to the Susquehanna nuclear plant — 2.5 gigawatts of nuclear power. 960 megawatt campus. $650 million deal.

Nuclear is the only zero-carbon power source that runs 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, at 90%+ capacity factor. That perfectly matches data center demand.

But FERC rejected the Amazon deal. Why? Because if a data center locks up dedicated nuclear power, it potentially reduces supply for everyone else. It's the cost-shifting debate: should one industry get priority access to the grid's most reliable power?

---

## SECTION 5: WHAT IF PJM FAILS? (6:00 - 7:30)

[Serious tone shift]

Let's talk about what happens if the grid can't keep up.

Texas gives us the analog. Winter Storm Uri, February 2021. Grid collapsed. Millions without power for days. 246 people died. $195 billion in economic damage.

PJM serves more people than ERCOT. A comparable failure would affect hospitals, water treatment, transportation, financial systems, and millions of homes across 13 states.

And PJM is interconnected with neighboring grids. A failure doesn't stay contained — it cascades.

The solutions exist: battery storage, nuclear restarts, transmission upgrades, demand response, interconnection reform. But they all require time, money, and political will.

---

## CLOSING (7:30 - 8:30)

[Direct to camera]

Here's the uncomfortable truth. We are building the most power-hungry technology in human history on a grid designed for a fundamentally different world.

As a data center engineer, I depend on grid reliability every single day. Our UPS systems buy us hours, not days. If the grid fails, we fail.

The data center industry cannot be a free rider on public infrastructure. If we consume 40% of the growth, we should contribute proportionally to the grid investment required to support it.

I'm Bagus Dwi Permana, Engineering Operations Manager. Thanks for watching.
