There's a power grid you've probably never heard of that serves 65 million people across 13 states — from Virginia to Illinois. It's called PJM Interconnection, and it's heading for a capacity crisis.

Here's what's happening: by 2027, PJM may not have enough electricity to meet demand. The projected shortfall is 6 gigawatts. To put that in perspective, 6 GW could power about 4.5 million homes.

Why? Three things are colliding at once.

First, about 40 gigawatts of coal and gas power plants are retiring or at risk of retirement by 2030. These plants are old, uneconomical, or can't meet new environmental regulations.

Second, data centers are consuming an enormous amount of new power. They account for 40% of PJM's projected load growth. In Virginia alone, Dominion Energy has received data center power requests totaling 30 gigawatts — that's more than its entire existing system handles during peak demand.

Third, replacements can't be built fast enough. Solar and wind are great, but they don't generate power 24/7. And the queue of new projects waiting for grid connection is 250+ gigawatts long, with an average wait of 4-5 years.

How serious is this? In December 2022, a winter storm caused PJM to lose 46 gigawatts of generation capacity. Rolling blackouts were minutes away. That was a preview.

The market already knows. PJM's capacity auction prices spiked 800% in one year — from $28 to $269 per megawatt-day. Total procurement cost jumped from $2.2 billion to $14.7 billion. Your electricity bill is about to reflect that.

Meanwhile, Microsoft is spending $1.6 billion to restart Three Mile Island's nuclear reactor for data center power. Amazon bought a campus next to another nuclear plant. The race for reliable power is reshaping the energy landscape.
