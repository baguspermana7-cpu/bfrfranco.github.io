QUORA ANSWER DRAFT
==================
Target Question: "Does zero incidents mean a facility is safe?"
(Also fits: "What are weak signals in operations?", "What is the difference between Safety-I and Safety-II?", "How to detect problems before they become incidents?")

---

ANSWER:

No. And this is one of the most dangerous assumptions in facility management.

"We had zero incidents last quarter" gets celebrated in board meetings. It shouldn't. Here's why:

A facility with zero incidents might be genuinely safe. Or it might be:
- Underreporting (culture of blame suppresses near-miss reports)
- Lucky (the failure conditions exist but haven't aligned yet)
- Blind (monitoring gaps mean incidents happen but aren't detected)
- Drifting (standards are slowly eroding but haven't crossed the threshold yet)

The aviation industry learned this lesson decades ago. They stopped using accident rates as the primary safety metric and started tracking precursor events, reporting culture health, and system margin indicators.

Data centers need the same shift.

Weak signals that predict incidents 6-12 months before they happen:
- Increasing number of "nuisance alarms" being disabled
- Rising proportion of reactive vs. planned maintenance
- Spare parts inventory below minimum stock levels
- Key person dependencies (only one person knows how to operate critical systems)
- Procedure workarounds becoming normalized

I call these "organizational vital signs." They're measurable, they're predictive, and they're almost never tracked.
