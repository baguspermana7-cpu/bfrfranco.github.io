QUORA ANSWER DRAFT
==================
Target Question: "Why do root cause analyses fail to prevent repeat incidents?"
(Also fits: "How to do effective RCA?", "Why do the same problems keep happening after incident reviews?", "What is organizational learning in operations?")

---

ANSWER:

I've sat through hundreds of RCA meetings. Here's the pattern that repeats 80% of the time:

Incident happens. Team investigates. Root cause identified. Corrective actions assigned. Report filed. Everyone moves on.

Six months later, same failure mode, different equipment. Why?

Because most RCAs stop at the proximate cause and never reach the systemic cause.

"The UPS failed because the capacitor degraded" is a proximate cause. Why did the capacitor degrade? Because the replacement cycle was 7 years instead of the OEM-recommended 5 years. Why? Because the maintenance budget was cut 15% last year. Why? Because the operations team couldn't quantify the risk of deferral in terms finance understood.

That last "why" is where the real fix lives — and it requires design authority, not just investigation authority.

The gap I see in most organizations: the people who investigate incidents have no authority to change the systems that produced them. They can recommend. They can escalate. They can write reports. But they cannot change the maintenance budget, the staffing model, the vendor contract, or the design standard.

Without design authority, RCA becomes theater. You document the finding, assign an action to "review the procedure," and the organizational structure that created the problem remains untouched.

Three fixes that actually work:
1. RCA teams must include someone with budget authority
2. Corrective actions must target the system, not just the component
3. Track recurrence rates — if the same failure mode appears 3 times, escalate to design-level change
