## SECTION 1: WHY DOES AI NEED HVAC ENGINEERS? (0:30 - 2:00)

[B-roll: data center cooling systems, server racks, chillers]

Here's something most people don't realize. Every time you use ChatGPT, every AI-generated image, every self-driving car decision — that computation happens on physical hardware. GPUs running at full load, generating enormous heat, inside buildings that consume as much electricity as small cities.

Those buildings need cooling systems. Power distribution. Fire suppression. Physical maintenance. 24/7, 365.

The AI boom isn't just creating software jobs. It's creating an unprecedented demand for the people who build and maintain the physical infrastructure.

Randstad analyzed over 50 million job postings and found skilled trades demand is growing three times faster than professional roles. Not slightly faster. Three times.

[Text overlay]
- HVAC: +67%
- Robotic Tech: +107%
- Electricians: +18%
- Construction: +30%

And the CEO of BlackRock — the world's largest asset manager — said this at a conference: "We're going to run out of electricians that we need to build out AI data centers."

Microsoft's president called it the single biggest challenge for data center expansion.

## SECTION 2: THE SILVER TSUNAMI (2:00 - 3:30)

[Graphics: age demographics, retirement projections]

Now here's what makes this really urgent.

The average data center engineer is about 60 years old. Let that sink in.

32 percent of the workforce is over 60. Only 16 percent is under 30. That's a 2-to-1 ratio of people leaving versus people entering.

23,000 experienced workers retire from data centers every single year. And the industry is trying to grow from 500,000 to 650,000 jobs at the same time.

340,000 positions are projected to be unfilled by the end of 2026.

[Text overlay: "Silver Tsunami"]
- 32% over 60
- 16% under 30
- 23,000 retiring/year
- 340,000 unfilled positions by 2026

This is a supply and demand mismatch that directly benefits anyone willing to enter the field.

## SECTION 3: THE MONEY (3:30 - 5:30)

[Salary graphics, progression chart]

Let's talk about what these jobs actually pay. And remember — no four-year degree required for any of these.

Within three to five years, mid-level: $68,000 to $84,000.

Senior technician: $105,000 to $142,000.

Now here's where it gets interesting. If you specialize in liquid cooling — the technology that keeps AI GPUs from overheating — you can earn $90,000 to $160,000.

AI infrastructure specialist: $140,000 to $200,000.

Data center director: $187,000 average.

## SECTION 4: HOW TO GET IN (5:30 - 7:00)

[B-roll: training programs, classrooms, certification logos]

So how do you actually get into this career?

Step one: Get your foundational certifications. BICSI Installer 1 for cabling. OSHA 10 for safety. CompTIA ITF+ if you want the IT side. That's 6 to 12 months of training.

Step two: Apply to one of the major training programs.

Microsoft runs a Datacenter Academy with hands-on training at their actual facilities. Google has an 18-month paid apprenticeship — they're accepting applications right now. Amazon has Career Choice, which funds IT Infrastructure Specialist training. Equinix has a military SkillBridge program for veterans.

And here's the big one: BlackRock — the company that manages 10 trillion dollars in assets — just committed $100 million to train 50,000 skilled trades workers. A hundred million dollars. Specifically targeting electricians, HVAC techs, and infrastructure workers.

Northern Virginia Community College offers the first accredited two-year degree in Data Center Operations. That program bundles your BICSI and OSHA certifications into the degree.

[Text overlay: Entry Path]
1. Certs: BICSI + OSHA (6 months)
2. Program: MS/Google/Amazon/NOVA (6-18 months)
3. Entry role: $45K-$57K
4. Specialize: liquid cooling, GPU ops
5. $100K+ in 3-5 years

## SECTION 5: THE DIVERSITY OPPORTUNITY (7:00 - 8:00)

One more thing that needs to be said.

50 percent of data centers have less than 5 percent women on staff. The average worker is 60 years old. Nearly one in four facilities has zero women in operations.

This industry has a massive diversity problem. And that's actually an opportunity.

Companies are actively looking to diversify their workforce. Programs are being built specifically to recruit underrepresented groups. If you're someone who wouldn't traditionally have considered data center operations — this door is wide open right now.
