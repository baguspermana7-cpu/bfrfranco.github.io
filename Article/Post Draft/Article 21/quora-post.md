# Quora Post

## Target Questions
- "Why are tech companies investing in nuclear power for AI?"
- "Can nuclear SMRs power data centers?"
- "What is a Small Modular Reactor?"
- "Will nuclear energy solve AI's power problem?"
- "Why does AI need so much electricity?"

---

## Answer

I have been a data center engineer for over 12 years. I operate the cooling and power systems that keep these facilities running. When five of the world's largest technology companies simultaneously turned to nuclear energy for AI in 2024-2025, I spent weeks analyzing every deal and every reactor design.

Here is why this is happening and what it actually means.

The Problem: AI data centers need enormous amounts of continuous power. A single hyperscale AI training cluster can consume 1 GW -- equivalent to a city of 750,000 homes. The IEA projects global data center electricity demand will double by 2030. The US grid interconnection queue has over 2,600 GW of projects waiting with 5+ year average delays.

Nuclear delivers what AI actually needs: 24/7 carbon-free baseload power at greater than 90% capacity factor. Solar averages about 25%. Wind about 35%. You cannot pause an AI training run that costs millions of dollars because the sun went down.

The Deals:

1. Microsoft: $1.6 billion to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1 (835 MW), 20-year PPA, target 2027.

2. Amazon: 1,920 MW nuclear PPA with Talen Energy through 2042, plus a 320-960 MW small modular reactor fleet with X-energy.

3. Google: 500 MW of advanced Kairos Power reactors by 2035, including the first Gen IV reactor PPA signed by a US utility.

4. Meta: 6.6 GW across three vendors (Vistra, TerraPower, Oklo) -- the largest nuclear commitment in tech history.

5. Oracle: Three SMRs for a 1+ GW data center campus.

Combined: over 11.8 GW of nuclear capacity committed.

What is a Small Modular Reactor? An SMR is a nuclear reactor with output below 300 MWe, designed for factory fabrication and modular deployment. Instead of building one massive plant, you deploy multiple smaller modules and scale up as demand grows. Key designs competing for the data center market include:

- NuScale VOYGR: 77 MWe per module, light water reactor, NRC certified
- GE-Hitachi BWRX-300: 300 MWe, under construction in Canada
- X-energy Xe-100: 80 MWe, helium-cooled (no water needed), backed by Amazon
- TerraPower Natrium: 345-500 MW, sodium-cooled with molten salt storage, broke ground June 2024
- Kairos Power Hermes: Molten salt cooled, backed by Google
- Oklo Aurora: 75 MWe, sodium-cooled, backed by Meta

The Honest Assessment: No commercial SMR has ever powered a data center. NuScale's flagship project escalated from $3.6B to $9.3B before cancellation. The only recent US nuclear construction (Vogtle) ran 7 years late and $21B over budget.

But Big Tech has something traditional utilities did not: combined cash reserves exceeding $300 billion, multi-decade PPAs providing revenue certainty, and a calculation that lost AI revenue from power constraints exceeds any reactor cost overrun.

My timeline: Microsoft's TMI restart in 2027 is highly likely (existing reactor). The first commercial SMR operations targeting data centers will not arrive before 2030. This is a 2030s solution being planned today.
