## Body

In September 2024, Microsoft signed a $1.6 billion deal to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1 -- the nuclear plant whose name has been synonymous with nuclear fear since 1979. The entire 835 MW output would power AI data centers.

Within weeks, Amazon, Google, Meta, and Oracle followed with their own nuclear announcements. By early 2026, Big Tech had collectively committed to over 11.8 GW of nuclear capacity. That is roughly 12 traditional nuclear power plants worth of energy, all earmarked for artificial intelligence.

As a data center engineer with over 12 years in critical infrastructure operations, I have operated cooling towers, specified power systems, and watched the AI power demand curve go vertical. The nuclear pivot is both entirely logical and deeply uncertain.

### The Logic

AI training workloads run 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, for weeks or months continuously. You cannot pause a training run because the sun went down.

Nuclear power has a capacity factor above 90%. Solar averages about 25%. Wind about 35%. For a 500 MW AI campus that needs continuous power, nuclear is the only carbon-free option that delivers around the clock without weather dependency.

The demand numbers are staggering. The IEA projects global data center electricity will double from 460 TWh to 945 TWh by 2030. Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory found US data centers consumed 176 TWh in 2023 -- 4.4% of total US electricity -- and could reach 325-580 TWh by 2028.

Meanwhile, the US grid interconnection queue has over 2,600 GW of projects waiting with average wait times exceeding five years. PJM, the largest US grid operator, projects a 6 GW reliability shortfall by 2027.

Nuclear co-location bypasses the queue entirely.

### The Deals

Microsoft's $1.6B to restart Three Mile Island (835 MW, 20-year PPA, target 2027) is the most likely near-term success because it is restarting an existing reactor, not building something new.

Amazon has the most diversified nuclear portfolio: a 1,920 MW PPA with Talen Energy's Susquehanna nuclear plant running through 2042, plus a 320-960 MW SMR fleet with X-energy (the Cascade project in Washington state).

Google partnered with Kairos Power for 500 MW of advanced molten-salt reactors by 2035. The first 50 MW PPA via TVA at Oak Ridge is the first Gen IV reactor agreement signed by a US utility.

Meta made the biggest single announcement: 6.6 GW across three vendors -- Vistra (2.1+ GW existing), TerraPower (690 MW new build), and Oklo (pre-construction 2026). All to power the Prometheus AI Supercluster in Ohio.

### The Cost Problem

This is where engineering reality intrudes.

NuScale's Carbon Free Power Project -- the poster child for SMR deployment -- saw costs escalate from $3.6 billion to $9.3 billion, a 158% increase. The target LCOE went from $55/MWh to $89/MWh. It was cancelled in November 2023.

The only new US nuclear construction in decades, Vogtle Units 3 and 4, ran seven years late and ballooned from $14 billion to $35 billion.

Canada's OPG BWRX-300, the furthest-advanced Western SMR, has a first-unit budget of US $5.6 billion for a 300 MW reactor.

Big Tech argues they are different customers. Combined cash reserves exceeding $300 billion. Twenty-year PPAs providing revenue certainty. And a calculation that the cost of not having power -- measured in lost AI training revenue -- exceeds any reactor cost overrun.

Whether that argument holds through construction delays and budget escalations remains to be seen.

### What I Am Watching

1. OPG Darlington BWRX-300 construction progress. This is the bellwether for whether SMR economics are real. Construction began May 2025, targeting 2029-2030.

2. TerraPower Natrium operating license timeline. The application expected 2027-2028 will test whether NRC can process advanced reactor licenses at the speed the industry needs.

3. The water question. Some SMR designs (X-energy Xe-100, TerraPower Natrium) use air cooling, eliminating water dependency. An air-cooled SMR co-located with a liquid-cooled data center could solve both the power AND water sustainability challenges.

4. The time gap. AI demand is growing at 15% per year now. SMRs will not deliver meaningful capacity until 2030 at the earliest. What fills the 4-5 year gap?

### The Bottom Line

The nuclear bet is enormous, unprecedented, and not guaranteed to work on the timeline or at the cost that Big Tech needs. But the alternative -- a future where AI progress is constrained by power availability -- is a risk these companies have decided they cannot accept.

Whether the engineering delivers will be one of the defining infrastructure stories of the next decade.
