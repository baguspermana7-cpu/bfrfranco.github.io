Could your household survive 72 hours without electricity, running water, or access to a grocery store?

Over 20 nations are now telling their citizens to prepare for exactly that scenario. Sweden distributes preparedness brochures to every household. Finland maintains Cold War-era shelters for its entire population. Poland passed a law mandating 72-hour household readiness. Japan recommends two full weeks of supplies.

This is not hypothetical. Modern supply chains run on just-in-time logistics. The average supermarket holds about 3 days of inventory. Pharmacies hold even less. A single disruption to energy, transport, or communications can cascade into widespread shortages within hours.

The 72-hour window exists because that is typically how long it takes emergency services to reach full operational capacity after a major event. During those first 3 days, you are effectively on your own.

I built an interactive 72-hour survival calculator that generates a personalized preparedness checklist based on your household size, medical needs, climate zone, and pet situation. It covers water (1 gallon per person per day), food (non-perishable, calorie-dense), medications, first aid, communications, and shelter considerations.

The calculator also generates a full exportable preparedness report with risk assessment scores and supply gap analysis.

Whether the trigger is a natural disaster, infrastructure failure, or geopolitical disruption, the preparedness framework is the same. The question is not if you will need it, but when.
