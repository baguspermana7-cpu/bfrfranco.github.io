I built an open-source data center commissioning tracker and learned something about how RFS readiness actually works.

Most commissioning teams track Ready-for-Service using spreadsheets with color-coded status columns. It works fine when everything is on schedule. It breaks completely when it is not -- which is most of the time.

The fundamental problem: commissioning readiness is gate-driven, not percentage-driven. You cannot be "87% ready for RFS." You are either blocked or you are not. And the blockers cascade.

Here is what I mean. G4 (Functional Performance Tests) requires all individual component tests (G3) to be complete. If you have an unresolved critical defect on a UPS system from G3, that blocks G4. Which blocks G5 (Integrated Systems Tests). Which blocks G6 (Operational Readiness). Which blocks G7 (Turnover).

One defect. Four gates delayed. Weeks of schedule drag.

Spreadsheets do not model this. They show you a cell that says "87% complete" when what you actually need to know is: "This gate has 2 hard blockers and cannot progress until they are resolved."

So I built the RFS Readiness Workbench -- a browser-based tool that models commissioning as a gate progression system (G0 through G7) with:

- Hard blocker logic that physically prevents gate advancement
- A 7-term confidence formula that quantifies risk per gate per unit
- Drag-based forecasting that breaks delay into 7 components
- Evidence tracking per requirement (metadata only, no binary storage)
- Source confidence tagging (A through D) where only contractual and industry standard sources can auto-block gates
- 6 facility archetypes with calibrated baseline timing
- 99 requirements covering electrical, mechanical, fire, controls, operations, and commercial disciplines

No backend. Client-side only. JSON export/import. Free tier covers the core gate and defect workflow.
