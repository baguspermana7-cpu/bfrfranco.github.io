Question: How do you track data center commissioning readiness?

Most teams use spreadsheets. That works until the first critical defect shows up at G4 and nobody knows how it affects the G5 timeline.

The core problem is that commissioning readiness is not linear. It is gate-driven. Each gate (G0 through G7) has specific requirements, evidence items, and sign-off roles. A hard blocker at one gate prevents progression to the next. But spreadsheets do not model this cascade.

After working with several commissioning programs, I built an open tool that handles this: the RFS Readiness Workbench.

How it works:

You define your facility archetype (enterprise, colocation, hyperscale, etc.) and delivery units. The tool loads 99 baseline requirements across 8 gates covering electrical, mechanical, fire, controls, operations, and more.

For each delivery unit, the gate engine evaluates readiness by checking:
- Hard blockers first (open critical defects, missing prior gate approval, mandatory evidence gaps, unmet overlay requirements)
- Weighted requirement completion as a percentage
- Readiness caps (95% max if evidence is missing, 99% if sign-offs are missing)

Confidence is scored with 7 penalty terms. A fresh project starts at 100% and decreases as defects accumulate, evidence gaps remain, approvals are pending, retests are needed, or aging thresholds are exceeded.

The forecast engine calculates RFS timing per delivery unit using drag components: base days (calibrated by archetype) plus delays from blockers, evidence gaps, sign-off bottlenecks, retest requirements, witness scheduling, and overlay uncertainty. Project RFS is the critical path -- the worst-performing unit determines the date.

Everything runs in the browser. No backend, no data leaves your machine. You can export/import as JSON.

It is free to try: resistancezero.com/rfs-readiness-workbench.html
