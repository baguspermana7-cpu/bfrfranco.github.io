Title: The Invisible PFAS Problem in Data Centers Nobody Is Counting

---

The first time I opened a two-phase cooling system, I was handed a wrench and a procedure sheet. Nobody mentioned that what I was about to release into the room had a vapor pressure eight times higher than water, belonged to a chemical family linked to cancer and endocrine disruption, and would drift out of the facility with zero regulatory obligation to track it. That was true on my first day. It is still true today, across an industry running thousands of these systems globally.

The conversation about data centers and environmental harm tends to focus on power consumption — megawatts, carbon credits, PUE ratios. That is a real problem, but it is at least a measured one. What does not get measured, and almost never gets reported, is what happens during maintenance. When an engineer opens a sealed two-phase cooling loop to inspect hardware, swap components, or top off fluid levels, the fluid surface is exposed directly to the room. At 270 hPa vapor pressure, Novec 7000 — one of the most common PFAS-based dielectric coolants — does not evaporate slowly. It leaves fast. Facilities that perform this work weekly or monthly are generating episodic release events that dwarf what leaks from intact sealed systems by a factor of 20 to 30. There is no federal reporting requirement for this. The EPA's Toxic Release Inventory applies to manufacturers, not to the operators doing the handling.

What makes this harder to resolve is that the industry believes it has already found an answer. The transition to HFO-based alternatives — marketed as PFAS-free — has been underway since 3M announced its exit from PFAS manufacturing at the end of 2025. The leading replacement, Opteon 2P50, reads well on a product data sheet. What it does not advertise is that HFOs degrade in the environment into trifluoroacetic acid, a compound that persists in water, accumulates across aquatic systems, and sits outside the scope of virtually every current PFAS regulatory framework. The category changed. The persistence did not.

There is also a utilization problem that rarely gets discussed. Two-phase immersion and direct-to-chip systems make thermal sense at rack densities above roughly 100 kilowatts. Less than five percent of data centers operate at that level. The rest adopted the technology during a period when advanced cooling became a competitive signal rather than an operational requirement — and in doing so, took on disposal liability currently estimated at eight to fifteen dollars per liter, with no line item on their balance sheets to show for it. A facility holding 100,000 liters of PFAS coolant is carrying approximately one million dollars in future disposal cost that most finance teams have not modeled.
