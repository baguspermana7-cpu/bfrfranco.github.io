Are traditional websites really dying?

I spent weeks researching this question by looking at actual traffic data since 2020 rather than relying on speculation.

The short answer: it depends on what kind of website you're talking about.

Static informational websites -- the kind that just present text and images answering common questions -- are absolutely losing ground. Google's AI Overviews now answer many queries directly in search results. In sectors where these AI summaries appear, organic traffic to independent sites dropped 30-40%. Meanwhile, platforms like TikTok, Instagram, and Reddit capture attention that previously flowed to traditional websites.

But here's what most "websites are dead" arguments miss entirely: certain types of websites are growing faster than ever. Interactive tools (calculators, configurators, dashboards), real-time data platforms, and community-driven content sites are thriving because they offer something AI summaries and social platforms cannot replicate.

The web isn't dying. It's splitting into two categories:

1. Content-only sites that can be summarized by AI -- these are declining
2. Interactive, tool-driven, community-powered sites -- these are growing

Three forces are driving this split:
- AI-generated answers replacing click-through traffic from search engines
- Walled-garden platforms capturing user attention within their ecosystems
- The economics of independent publishing becoming harder each year

The question isn't whether websites are dying. It's which ones survive and what replaces the rest.
