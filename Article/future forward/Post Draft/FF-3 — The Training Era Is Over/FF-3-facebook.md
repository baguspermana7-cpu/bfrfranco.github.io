Here's something most people don't realize about AI costs:

Training ChatGPT reportedly cost over $100 million. That sounds like the expensive part, right? But training is a one-time cost — you build the model once.

The real expense is inference — actually running the model every time someone asks a question. And right now, two-thirds of all AI compute globally goes to inference, not training. Every single ChatGPT query, every AI-generated image, every code suggestion — that's inference, running 24/7 across thousands of GPUs.

API prices dropped 99.5% in two years. Yet AI infrastructure spending hit $450 BILLION in 2025.

Why? Because cheaper AI means more people use it. More usage means more servers, more GPUs, more power. By 2030, AI inference alone is projected to consume 93.3 gigawatts of electricity — more than many countries use in total.

The hardware companies know this. NVIDIA's newest chips are designed for inference, not training. Groq was just acquired for $20 billion because their chip does inference 10x faster than anything else. Even Comcast is running AI on edge servers instead of the cloud and cutting costs by 76%.

What does this mean for the future? By 2028, inference compute will be like electricity — metered, ubiquitous, and invisible. The companies that figure out inference economics will define the next decade of AI.
