The Engineer Shortage Is Fake — Here Is Who Made It

Every few months, a new report lands confirming what the industry already knows: hundreds of thousands of data center roles are unfilled, retirements are accelerating, and the next generation of critical infrastructure engineers simply is not there. The framing is always the same. Shortage. Gap. Crisis. A problem that seems to have descended on the industry from nowhere, like weather.

It didn't come from nowhere.

The 340,000 unfilled positions are real. So is the 58% of operators who say they are struggling to hire. But a closer look at how those companies actually hire — and whether they invest in developing talent — tells a more uncomfortable story. Only 1% of data center employers are willing to train entry-level candidates. Job descriptions routinely demand five or more years of experience for roles in GPU infrastructure and liquid cooling systems that did not meaningfully exist five years ago. Compensation for mid-level roles runs 15 to 30 percent below comparable positions in cloud or software. And entry-level hiring has collapsed by 25 to 60 percent since 2020, depending on the region.

These are not market forces. These are choices.

When 32% of your existing workforce is over 60 and no hiring pipeline exists to replace them, the math becomes stark. Each unplanned outage costs over $300,000 per hour. The cost of a structured apprenticeship program is a rounding error by comparison. Singapore and Germany have already figured this out — both implemented targeted workforce development programs and are outpacing the US and UK in closing their gaps. The difference is not talent density. It is policy intent.
