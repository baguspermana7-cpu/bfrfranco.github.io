340,000 data center roles are unfilled — and the industry wants you to believe there just aren't enough engineers.

I don't buy it. Here's what the data actually shows:

- Only 1% of data center employers are willing to train entry-level hires. One percent. The other 99% post job descriptions requiring 5+ years of experience for roles that barely existed five years ago, then complain the talent pool is too shallow.

- Entry-level hiring collapsed between 25-60% since 2020, depending on the region. Companies pulled back on junior hiring precisely when they needed to be building the next generation of talent.

- 32% of the current workforce is over 60. That's not a looming problem — it's a ticking clock. Retirements are accelerating, and there's no bench to replace them.

- New roles in GPU infrastructure and liquid cooling are paying $160,000-$230,000 a year. Those positions sit empty for months while the same companies decline to run apprenticeship programs or partner with technical schools.

- A single outage costs $300,000+ per hour. Training a junior engineer costs a fraction of that. The math isn't complicated.

Compare this to Singapore and Germany, where targeted workforce programs and government-industry partnerships have meaningfully closed their gaps. The difference isn't geography — it's intent.

The shortage is real in the sense that seats are empty. But it's manufactured in the sense that it didn't have to be. Unrealistic job descriptions, below-market compensation, and near-zero training investment are policy decisions, not market forces.
