Why Post-Incident RCA Fails Without Design Authority

Root cause analysis as ritual versus RCA as a learning and redesign engine.

9 min read November 15, 2025 Bagus Dwi Permana

Abstract

Root Cause Analysis (RCA) is widely practiced in data centers, yet repeated incidents continue to occur with familiar patterns. This article argues that the failure is not analytical capability, but structural: RCA is performed without design authority. When findings cannot modify system architecture, workflows, or decision boundaries, RCA becomes a reporting ritual rather than a learning mechanism.

"Most RCAs are technically correct. Most RCAs also change nothing. If the same incident returns in a new form, the organization has not failed to analyze. It has failed to redesign."

1. RCA Popularity and Its Paradox

RCA is one of the most standardized practices in critical environments. Templates, fishbone diagrams, and timelines are well understood. Yet recurrence rates remain high. The paradox is clear: analysis quality increases, but system behavior does not change.

2. RCA as Ritualized Compliance

In many organizations, RCA primarily serves governance needs: to demonstrate diligence, to satisfy audit expectations, to close incident tickets. This transforms RCA into a ritual. The output is documentation, not system change.

The Trap: RCA that does not alter control structures cannot address system-level causes. The deeper the system issue, the less effective isolated fixes become.

3. Design Authority as the Missing Control

Design authority is the ability to modify system constraints: architecture, interfaces, decision rights, and standards. Without design authority:

  • Findings are downgraded to recommendations
  • Systemic issues are reframed as human error
  • Corrective actions focus on reminders and retraining

Learning stops at the document boundary.

5. Systems Thinking Perspective

Nancy Leveson emphasizes that accidents emerge from inadequate control, not component failure. RCA that does not alter control structures cannot address system-level causes. The deeper the system issue, the less effective isolated fixes become.

6. Case Pattern: RCA Without Redesign

Across repeated events, a familiar pattern appears:

  1. Incident occurs
  2. RCA identifies procedural deviation
  3. Training is reinforced
  4. Similar incident reappears months later

The loop closes without modifying the conditions that made deviation likely.

7. Human Factors and Normalization

From a human factors view, people adapt to make work succeed. If RCA punishes adaptation instead of redesigning constraints, it encourages workarounds and silence. Safety-II reframes this: success and failure share the same mechanisms.

8. What Effective RCA Enables

Effective RCA has one defining property: it can change the system

  • Modifying alarm logic
  • Redefining authority boundaries
  • Changing maintenance workflows
  • Redesigning interfaces
  • Retiring unsafe assumptions

Without this power, RCA cannot mature.

9. Designing an RCA-to-Design Pipeline

Resilient organizations formalize the transition from RCA to redesign:

  • Explicit classification of findings (local vs systemic)
  • Pre-approved redesign scopes
  • Design review ownership
  • Change authority embedded in ops leadership

RCA becomes input, not endpoint.

10. Conclusion

"RCA does not fail because teams are incompetent. It fails because organizations separate analysis from authority. When RCA gains the power to redesign, learning becomes real and recurrence declines."
